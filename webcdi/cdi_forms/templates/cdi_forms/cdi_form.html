    In this section of the assessment, you will be asked about the kinds of signs, phrases, words, and gestures that your child understands. When we ask if your child "understands" or "understands and says" a word, there are a lot of possible meanings! Here are some examples of what we mean.

    When it comes to "understanding" a word:

    Just hearing the word "ball" is NOT enough to prove that your child "understands" that word.

    However, if you think your child would look for a ball if they heard the word "ball", then your child "understands" the word "ball".

    When it comes to "understanding and saying" a word:

    Your child says "ball" in the absence of a ball, that does NOT mean that your child "understands AND says" the word "ball".

    In that same vein, teaching your child to say "ball" does NOT mean that your child "understands AND says" the word "ball".

    It's fine if your child cannot say the whole word! Saying "raff" instead of "giraffe" can still mean that your child "understands and says" a word in some cases.

    However if you think your child would say "ball" in the presence of a ball, then your child "understands and says" that word.
    {% elif instrument_name == "English_WS" %} {% load staticfiles %}

    In this section of the assessment, you will be asked about the kinds of words and sentences that your child says. When we ask if your child "says" a word, there can be more than one meaning! Here are some examples of what we mean:


    Your child says "ba" or "ball" in the absence of a ball, that does NOT mean that your child "says" the word "ball".

    In that same vein, teaching your child to say "ba" or "ball" does NOT mean that your child "says" the word "ball".

    However if you think your child would say "ba" or "ball" in the presence of a ball, then your child "says" that word.

    It's fine if your child cannot say the whole word! Saying "raff" instead of "giraffe" can still mean that your child "says" a word in some cases.
